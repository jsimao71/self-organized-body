# Verified Literature and Reading Paths

This file grounds the manuscript in established work without implying that the cited authors endorse the book's stronger hypotheses.

## Systems biology, causal levels, and organization

1. Noble, D. (2012). **A theory of biological relativity: no privileged level of causation.** *Interface Focus*, 2(1), 55–64. https://doi.org/10.1098/rsfs.2011.0067  
   Relevance: formal statement that biological causation cannot be assigned universally to one privileged level.

2. Ellis, G. F. R. (2012). **Top-down causation and emergence: some comments on mechanisms.** *Interface Focus*, 2(1), 126–140. https://doi.org/10.1098/rsfs.2011.0062  
   Relevance: mechanisms through which higher-level organization constrains lower-level dynamics.

3. Maturana, H. R., & Varela, F. J. (1980). **Autopoiesis and Cognition.** Springer. https://doi.org/10.1007/978-94-009-8947-4  
   Relevance: living systems as self-producing organizations.

4. Kauffman, S. A. (1993). **The Origins of Order.** Oxford University Press.  
   Relevance: self-organization and attractor dynamics in biological systems.

5. Friston, K. (2010). **The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?** *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 11, 127–138. https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn2787  
   Relevance: hierarchical prediction, action, and self-maintaining dynamics.

## Morphogenesis, bioelectricity, and target states

6. Levin, M. (2021). **Bioelectric signaling: Reprogrammable circuits underlying embryogenesis, regeneration, and cancer.** *Cell*, 184(8), 1971–1989. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2021.02.034  
   Relevance: distributed control of anatomy and pattern regulation.

7. Pezzulo, G., & Levin, M. (2016). **Top-down models in biology: explanation and control of complex living systems above the molecular level.** *Journal of the Royal Society Interface*, 13, 20160555. https://doi.org/10.1098/rsif.2016.0555  
   Relevance: goal-directed and top-down descriptions of living systems.

## Aging and resilience

8. López-Otín, C., et al. (2023). **Hallmarks of aging: An expanding universe.** *Cell*, 186(2), 243–278. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2022.11.001  
   Relevance: current mainstream taxonomy of interacting aging processes.

9. López-Otín, C., et al. (2013). **The Hallmarks of Aging.** *Cell*, 153(6), 1194–1217. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2013.05.039

10. Kirkwood, T. B. L. (2005). **Understanding the odd science of aging.** *Cell*, 120(4), 437–447. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2005.01.027  
    Relevance: maintenance, trade-offs, and evolutionary explanations.

11. Gijzel, S. M. W., et al. (2017). **Resilience in clinical care: getting a grip on the recovery potential of older adults.** *Journal of the American Geriatrics Society*, 65(12), 2650–2657. https://doi.org/10.1111/jgs.15014  
    Relevance: recovery dynamics rather than static health measures.

## Reprogramming, regeneration, and cell plasticity

12. Lu, Y., et al. (2020). **Reprogramming to recover youthful epigenetic information and restore vision.** *Nature*, 588, 124–129. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-020-2975-4  
    Relevance: OSK expression in mouse retinal ganglion cells, axonal regeneration, and partial recovery of visual function.

13. Merrell, A. J., & Stanger, B. Z. (2016). **Adult cell plasticity in vivo: de-differentiation and transdifferentiation are back in style.** *Nature Reviews Molecular Cell Biology*, 17, 413–425. https://doi.org/10.1038/nrm.2016.24  
    Relevance: mature cell states can be less irreversible than classical lineage diagrams imply.

14. Tata, P. R., & Rajagopal, J. (2016). **Cellular plasticity: 1712 to the present day.** *Current Opinion in Cell Biology*, 43, 46–54. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ceb.2016.07.005

15. Yamanaka, S., & Blau, H. M. (2010). **Nuclear reprogramming to a pluripotent state by three approaches.** *Nature*, 465, 704–712. https://doi.org/10.1038/nature09229

16. Yücel, A. D., et al. (2024). **The long and winding road of reprogramming-induced rejuvenation.** *Nature Communications*, 15, 2714. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-46020-5  
    Relevance: opportunities, tissue differences, and safety constraints of partial reprogramming.

## Motor control, variability, and embodied regulation

20. Todorov, E., & Jordan, M. I. (2002). **Optimal feedback control as a theory of motor coordination.** *Nature Neuroscience*, 5, 1226–1235. https://doi.org/10.1038/nn963

21. Latash, M. L. (2012). **The bliss (not the problem) of motor abundance.** *Experimental Brain Research*, 217, 1–5. https://doi.org/10.1007/s00221-012-3000-4  
    Relevance: degrees of freedom can support flexible coordination rather than merely create a control problem.

22. Newell, K. M. (1986). **Constraints on the development of coordination.** In *Motor Development in Children*.  
    Relevance: movement emerges from organism, task, and environmental constraints.

## Fasting, autophagy, and maintenance

26. Longo, V. D., & Mattson, M. P. (2014). **Fasting: molecular mechanisms and clinical applications.** *Cell Metabolism*, 19(2), 181–192. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2013.12.008

27. Levine, B., & Kroemer, G. (2019). **Biological functions of autophagy genes: a disease perspective.** *Cell*, 176(1–2), 11–42. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2018.09.048  
    Relevance: autophagy is intracellular maintenance, not a synonym for whole-cell senolysis.

## Reading discipline

These references support mechanisms and conceptual ancestry. They do **not** jointly prove the book's central hypothesis. Each new edition should move references from this reading path into precise inline citations tied to individual claims.
